A personal note on photography

Against the obvious

A personal note on the kinds of photographs I return to, and why

Against the obvious

There is a kind of photograph that gives itself away at once. Blue sky. Ice-capped mountains. An azure lake in the foreground. Everything is clear, everything is legible, and whatever pleasure the image has to offer is exhausted in a single glance. I can admire such photographs, but I rarely return to them.

What I resist is not beauty itself but literalness. A poem is not made by listing beautiful things, and a photograph is not made memorable simply because what stood before the lens was already impressive. Anyone can describe a scene. What matters is whether the image becomes more than description. The photographs I care about do not merely record what was there. They alter the terms on which the world is seen.

The difference between description and poetry is the difference between being told about a feeling and having it.

This is one of the things I have learned from Robert Adams. His photographs of the American West refuse easy grandeur. They do not deny beauty, but they place it under pressure: houses pressing into plains, mountains sharing the frame with sprawl, light falling on things that are not noble enough to deserve it and yet receive it anyway. That tension is what makes the image stay alive.

American Silence — National Gallery of Art

Fay Godwin does something similar in a different register. Her landscapes do not surrender to the picturesque. Moor, path, fence, coast, industrial edge: the land is never emptied into scenery. It carries weather, use, and memory. What I respond to in both Adams and Godwin is not simply style, but a moral and poetic seriousness. They remind me that beauty becomes interesting when it is complicated.

Ambiguity and return

Raymond Moore  ·  Josef Koudelka

I prefer photographs that do not yield themselves all at once. This is not because I value obscurity for its own sake. Ambiguity, when it matters, is not vagueness or confusion. It is what makes a second look necessary, and a third look possible. The image does not close after recognition; it continues.

Raymond Moore is one of the photographers who clarified this for me. His images can seem slight at first: a wall, a shore, a shape in mist, a patch of light. But they do not remain slight. The longer one stays with them, the more they disclose. Not by explaining themselves, but by teaching the eye how to attend. The subject is never only the thing photographed; it is the interval around it, the tone of the light, the precise feeling of a moment being irreversibly itself.

If Moore represents ambiguity at its most delicate, Josef Koudelka shows a harsher and larger form of it. In his Chaos photographs, scale wavers, orientation becomes uncertain, and the familiar markers that let us categorise a scene fall away. You are not merely looking at an image but finding your way through it. I value such photographs because they do not flatten experience into a message. They remain partly ahead of me.

The more you look, the more you learn — not because the image gives more, but because you bring more each time.
Koudelka — Pace Gallery

The human presence

James Ravilious  ·  Raymond Depardon

Some of the photographs I care about most are not ambiguous because they are visually obscure, but because people themselves are never fully knowable. A face, a gesture, a body at work, someone standing in a doorway or crossing a street: these can be entirely visible and still remain beyond summary. That, too, is one of photography's deepest powers.

James Ravilious is important to me for this reason. His photographs of rural Devon are rooted in ordinary life, labor, mud, weather, barns, faces, and afternoons that seem at first entirely comprehensible. Yet the longer one looks, the less these images feel like documents and the more they feel like encounters. A person is never reduced to a type. A life remains partly veiled. The image respects the opacity of other people.

Raymond Depardon, especially in the Glasgow photographs, moves with a different temperament but reaches something related. He does not force intimacy. He stands at a slight remove and lets the distance remain. Wet streets, subdued light, figures in passage or pause: the photographs do not explain the lives they show, and are stronger for that restraint. I find in work like this a model for how to photograph people without reducing them to evidence.

Depardon Interview — University of Glasgow

Making a world from the ordinary

Sergio Larrain  ·  John Gossage

The photographers I return to most often can make a world out of things that, in themselves, seem unremarkable. A stairway, a wall, a child running, a branch, a pool of water, a patch of ground. Nothing spectacular has to be present. What matters is the quality of attention that rearranges the ordinary until it becomes newly resonant.

Sergio Larrain does this with a kind of lyric swiftness. His streets feel real and dreamlike at once, as though a slight shift in angle or light had disclosed another order already hidden inside the visible world. John Gossage, in The Pond, arrives somewhere equally strange by slower means. Broken branches, mud, standing water: almost nothing that would conventionally ask for a photograph. Yet by the end of the sequence one has been taken somewhere unmistakable. The place is ordinary. The seeing is not.

To photograph the mundane is not to settle for it. It is to argue that this ordinary world is worth prolonged and serious attention.
Valparaíso series — Magnum Larrain: Wanderings — ICP New York The Pond — Smithsonian American Art Museum

What I'm actually looking for

What I want from a photograph is not information, and not even beauty in any simple sense. I want a way of seeing that turns fact into feeling, that leaves something unresolved, that allows the visible world to become larger than recognition. The photographs I return to are poetic rather than literal, suggestive rather than complete.

I want ambiguity, but not emptiness. I want human presence, but not explanation. I want the ordinary world, but altered by attention until it becomes more inhabitable, more mysterious, more worth staying with. Above all, I want an image that continues after first sight, that does not collapse into its own subject, that asks something of me in return for what it gives.

That, finally, is why I think of the photographs I admire as closer to poetry than to description. They do not simply tell me what was there. They make another experience possible inside what was there. They do not settle the world. They make it larger.

The camera is a machine for making facts. But facts, arranged with sufficient care and feeling, stop being merely factual. They become something else. They become true.
