Part Two — Color as Structure 77
Chapter Six

Color as
Collision

Harmony is one way colors can relate.
Collision is another — and often the more interesting one.

In the summer of 1905, André Derain arrived in the small fishing village of Collioure on the southern coast of France at the invitation of Henri Matisse. They were there to paint. What they produced over the following weeks — in landscapes, harbour views, and figure studies — constituted an act of chromatic violence so jarring that when the work was exhibited at the Salon d’Automne in Paris that autumn, a critic named Louis Vauxcelles walked into the gallery and, confronted by the canvases, reached for an insult. He called the painters les fauves: the wild beasts. The name stuck, and the movement it named lasted only five years before most of its practitioners moved on to other concerns. But the principle it established has not been superseded in the century since: that colors do not have to agree, and that their disagreement can be a structural force rather than a failure of taste.

The Fauvist insight, stripped to its essentials, is this: color has been assigned the job of describing surfaces, and it need not accept that job. A sky is not pink. Water is not yellow. A bridge is not deep blue. But a pink sky and yellow water and a blue bridge can coexist in a single image without lying — because the image is not about the sky and the water and the bridge. It is staging a relationship between pink and yellow and blue, and showing what that relationship does to the eye, the attention, the felt sense of the scene. The colors are not descriptive. They are structural. They do not represent the world. They organise the viewer’s passage through the image.

This is the lineage into which color photography’s most serious practitioners have entered, whether or not they would describe it that way. The photographs of Ernst Haas in the 1950s and 60s, and of Alex Webb from the 1980s onward, extend the Fauvist argument into the street — into the uncontrolled, unstageable world of available light, moving figures, and found color. What Derain could arrange on canvas, they must find. And finding it requires a particular quality of visual attention: one trained to see color not as an attribute of things but as a force in its own right, capable of collision, capable of structure, capable of carrying meaning before any subject has been identified.

✦

The word collision is chosen deliberately. Harmony is the more familiar ideal — colors that agree, that occupy adjacent positions on the color wheel, that produce a unified emotional register. Harmony is not wrong. It is simply the less interesting possibility. A harmonious image settles. The eye moves through it comfortably, and that comfort is the experience the image offers. There is nothing to resist, nothing to push back against. The colors have agreed in advance, and the agreement is the image’s primary statement.

Collision produces a different experience entirely. When two colors of opposing intensity and temperature are placed in direct adjacency — when a saturated red meets a deep blue, when a yellow of full intensity presses against a pink — neither yields. Each asserts its own visual weight. The eye cannot resolve the relationship into comfort. It must hold both simultaneously, register the tension between them, and find its way through the image not along a path of agreement but across a field of competing demands. The image does not settle. It vibrates.

Collision is not discord. It is the condition in which color stops being description and becomes argument — each hue pressing its force against every other, none of them willing to recede into mere background.

This vibration is not a side effect of chromatic collision. It is its purpose. The painter or photographer who uses color this way is not failing to achieve harmony. They are achieving something more demanding: an image in which the color relationships generate their own energy, independent of subject matter, independent of light, independent of everything except the specific combination of hues that has been assembled in the frame.

✦

Derain’s Charing Cross Bridge, London (1906) — the Thames in planes of yellow and green, the bridge a band of deep blue, the sky a hot saturated pink — is the painted textbook of this principle: no color describes its subject, and every color collides with its neighbour. But the argument of this chapter is that the same chromatic violence is available to the camera. So look, instead, at a photograph made five years later and half a world away. In 1911 Sergey Prokudin-Gorsky travelled the Russian empire with a camera that exposed three black-and-white plates in succession — through red, green, and blue filters, to be recombined into color — and in Bukhara he photographed the emirate’s last ruler, Alim Khan. Look at it before reading the analysis below. Let the colors register before the subject does.

Bukhara as Chromatic Argument

Begin with what the camera refused to do. A photograph, of all media, might be expected to render the Emir’s robe as a particular fabric in a particular light — to describe it. Instead the color arrives first and the cloth second. The robe is not a blue garment; it is blue itself, pushed to an intensity that has nothing to do with the modest reflectance of dyed silk and everything to do with chromatic structure.

The robe is electric cobalt, scattered with magenta irises and green leaves. It is the image’s anchor and its loudest voice, filling the lower half of the frame and refusing to recede. Against the bare plaster wall behind it the blue would merely be vivid. It is the doors that make it collide.

The carved doors are warm ochre — orange, the exact complement of the robe’s blue, flanking it on both sides. This is the collision the chapter is named for: not a clash of unrelated hues but the maximum tension of two colors that sit opposite each other on the wheel, each driving the other to a higher pitch. Cover the doors and the robe calms; restore them and it begins to vibrate.

And the collision repeats at smaller scale. The magenta irises embroidered across the cobalt are a second clash — warm pink on cold blue — rhyming with the larger opposition of robe and doors. The image is built, like Derain’s bridge, out of nested complementary tensions rather than out of description.

What makes this remarkable is the date. Prokudin-Gorsky exposed his filtered plates in 1911 — five years after the Charing Cross Bridge, and with a camera rather than a brush. The chromatic argument the Fauves made in paint was, it turns out, available to photography from the very beginning of color. The medium changed; the structure did not.

✦

The line from Derain to color street photography is not straight, but it is traceable. The crucial link is Ernst Haas, the Austrian-American photographer who in the 1950s began making color photographs of New York and other cities that used saturated, colliding hues in a way that had no precedent in photography and a clear one in painting. Haas was working at a moment when color photography was still considered, by most serious practitioners, to be either a commercial medium or a documentary one — capable of recording the world’s colors accurately, but not of making structural arguments with them. His images of neon signs, wet pavements, and crowds in motion demonstrated that this assumption was wrong. Color could be the primary carrier of meaning in a photograph. The subject need not determine the palette. The palette could determine the image.

What Haas understood, and what the photographers who followed him have extended, is that the street provides a continuous supply of chromatic collision if the eye is trained to find it. A red wall next to a blue door. A yellow taxi cutting across a field of grey pavement. An orange shirt moving through a crowd of black jackets. These are not compositional decisions. They are found conditions — accidents of urban color that occur thousands of times a day in any city and that most eyes pass over without registering. The photographer whose eye has been trained by Derain, or by Matisse, or simply by long attention to what color does in images, sees these collisions as structural opportunities. They stop. They frame. They wait for the elements to align in a way that makes the collision legible as argument rather than accident.

Haas was not working alone. In the same years, Helen Levitt — long admired for her tender black-and-white photographs of children and street life in New York — began photographing those same sidewalks in color, and so became one of the earliest artists to treat color street photography as serious art, two decades before Eggleston’s 1976 exhibition made the position respectable. Hers is the quiet end of the tradition. Where Webb would later structure his hues into open conflict, Levitt waited for them to fall, briefly and without violence, into relationship — the found rhyme of a pink dress against a green stoop, a red awning answering a child’s red sock. Collision is one register of color in the street; coincidence is another. Both rest on the same discovery — that color out there is structure, not decoration.

On Saturation and Its Responsibilities

Saturation — the intensity or purity of a color, its distance from grey — is the variable that makes collision possible. Two colors of low saturation placed in adjacency will not collide. They will merely coexist, their muted relationship producing an atmosphere rather than an argument. Full-saturation colors placed in adjacency cannot coexist passively. Each asserts itself. The eye is pulled in multiple directions simultaneously.

This means that the photographer who works with chromatic collision is making a commitment — not just to a palette, but to a level of visual intensity that the image must justify. A saturated collision that serves no structural purpose is simply garish: high energy, low meaning. The test is whether the collision is doing compositional work — whether the relationship between the colliding colors is generating something (weight, depth, rhythm, path) that the image would lack without it.

Derain passes this test. His pink sky is not merely vivid. It is structurally necessary: without it, the blue bridge would have nothing to collide against at the top of the composition, and the image’s vertical structure would collapse. Webb passes this test. His reds are not decorative. They are anchors — the point of maximum visual weight in images that would otherwise lack a centre of gravity.

Alex Webb’s photographs from Haiti, Mexico, Cuba, and the American South are the most sustained and rigorous extension of this tradition in contemporary photography. Working in conditions of intense tropical light — where shadows are deep and colors at their maximum saturation — he has built a body of work in which chromatic collision is the primary compositional tool. A red wall occupies an entire foreground plane. Against it, a dark figure moves in shadow. Through a gap in the architecture, a slice of blue sky or turquoise wall appears in the background. Three colors, three planes, no harmony. The image holds together not because the colors agree but because their disagreement has been structured — because the collision has been framed so that the tensions between planes are productive rather than merely violent.

What distinguishes Webb’s use of collision from simpler forms of chromatic intensity is the depth at which it operates. The colors do not merely sit side by side in a single plane. They are distributed across the image’s full spatial depth — foreground, middle ground, background — and the collision between them creates a path for the eye that moves through the image rather than across it. This is color doing the work of composition: organizing the viewer’s movement through a complex, layered space by means of chromatic relationships rather than spatial geometry. It is Derain’s bridge in the street — the same principle, the same intensity, the same refusal of description in favor of argument.

Further Looking
  • André Derain — London Paintings (National Gallery, London, 2005–06 exhibition catalogue) The most thorough account of the thirty London paintings made in 1906. The catalogue essays situate Derain’s chromatic choices within the broader Fauvist argument and compare his London to Monet’s earlier series on the same subject. The contrast is the education.
  • Ernst Haas — The Creation (1971) Haas’s most ambitious color project — a visual meditation on the Book of Genesis using saturated, abstract color photography. The work that most clearly demonstrated that color in photography could carry structural rather than merely descriptive weight.
  • Alex Webb — The Suffering of Light (2011) Thirty years of street photography collected in a single volume. The most comprehensive demonstration of color-as-collision in contemporary photography. Study the images that feel most difficult first — the ones in which the color relationships are most extreme. Those are the ones doing the most structural work.
  • Helen Levitt — Slide Show: The Color Photographs of Helen Levitt (powerHouse, 2005) Levitt’s color street work from 1959–60 and 1971–74, much of it unseen for decades. The quiet, lyrical counterpart to Haas and Webb — color found in the unposed life of the street rather than constructed against it. Among the earliest serious arguments that color photography could be art.
  • Matisse on Art, edited by Jack Flam (1995) Matisse’s own writings on color, collected and annotated. His “Notes of a Painter” (1908) contains the clearest account of why color must be understood structurally rather than descriptively — written at the moment Fauvism was giving way to the next set of problems, and all the more precise for that.
